# 中美首届人工智能政府间对话于瑞士日内瓦举行

2024 年 5 月 14 日，中美两国在瑞士日内瓦召开首届人工智能政府间对话会议。

此次会议是中美两国代表历经数月筹备的成果，其筹备工作的起点是 2023 年 11 月亚太经济合作组织（APEC）领导人非正式会议期间，中美元首会晤达成的相关具体共识。正如外界预期，本次对话并未形成具体决议、成果文件或联合声明，但两国政府均对此次交流观点的机会表示满意。

拜登政府正致力于为避免军事冲突构建 “防护栏”，美国官员将此类人工智能对话称作 “负责任管控竞争的重要组成部分”，认为其有助于维护全球安全。美国首位网络空间与数字政策无任所大使纳撒尼尔・C・菲克表示，这一人工智能沟通渠道 “将推动中美两国在其他领域存在竞争的同时，找到可以开展合作的契合点”。中美两国均参与了去年在英国举办的首届人工智能安全峰会（会议发表了中美共同签署的《布莱奇利宣言》），以及今年 5 月在韩国首尔举行的后续峰会。

美国国务卿安东尼・布林肯在上月于旧金山举办的 RSA 大会上发表演讲，阐述了美国当前针对新兴技术的政策立场，此次演讲的时间节点恰好与美国国务院发布新版《国际网络空间与数字政策战略》相契合。

美国官员表示，该战略的 “核心指导原则 —— 即其组织纲领，是与美国秉持共同价值观的盟友、合作伙伴、企业及公民社会行为体之间的‘数字团结’理念”。布林肯将 “数字团结” 定义为 “愿意为共同目标携手合作…… 助力合作伙伴提升能力以实现相互支持…… 在规范制定、监管措施到技术标准等各个层面开展协作”。美国官员称，这种对伙伴关系的重视 —— 美国国防部长劳埃德・奥斯汀近期在香格里拉对话会上的演讲也体现了这一点 —— 与威权政体所奉行的 “数字主权” 优先理念形成鲜明对比。

在日内瓦对话中，中方代表试图扭转美国不断收紧的先进技术对华转让限制。中国政府认为，美国针对可能具有军事或人工智能应用潜力的先进半导体实施的 “小院高墙” 出口限制政策，覆盖范围过宽、限制力度过大。中方转而提议，考虑开展中美人工智能相关的联合研发项目。

中国政府同时希望强化联合国在人工智能规范与规则制定中的作用，部分原因是为了避免美国科技企业或政府在该领域掌握决定性话语权。中国在《全球数字契约》联合国宣言谈判过程中也持这一立场，此举同时旨在争取那些缺乏头部人工智能企业的发展中国家的支持。尽管中美在联合国框架内存在合作，但美国官员的工作重心更多放在七国集团（G7），而中国和俄罗斯均非该集团成员。为争取全球南方国家的认同，布林肯等美国官员着重强调了其利用人工智能推动落实联合国可持续发展目标的积极愿景。

6 月 6 日，菲克在美国《华盛顿邮报》总部发表演讲时表示，美国有意继续与中国开展对话。例如，两国政府可以就前沿人工智能系统如何与人类价值观相契合交换信息。目前美国的政策重心更倾向于推动开发者作出 “自愿承诺”，对新技术开展严格的安全审查。菲克认为这种方式灵活性强，能够适应技术的快速迭代；不过未来美国也可能向中国政府采取的强监管模式靠拢。

尽管如此，此类对话的范围与影响力仍将受限。美国牵头制定了《负责任军事使用人工智能与自主系统政治宣言》，已有 50 余个国家签署该宣言，但中国拒绝签署。这份宣言需获得更多国家支持，才能成为具有约束力的国际行为准则。

美国不太可能放宽针对具有军事应用价值的本国技术的国家安全出口管制与投资限制。出于同样原因，美国官员将继续抵制开展技术研发合作项目，并可能限制中国获取美国开发的闭源人工智能模型。此外，美国还希望防范外国行为体利用人工智能技术干预美国选举。分析人士担忧，人工智能工具会降低网络操纵的实施门槛，同时提升其效果。例如，人工智能可生成更逼真的政治人物 “深度伪造” 内容，实现对特定人群的精准定向信息投放，还能让境外宣传内容显得更真实可信。

不仅如此，美国官员还将持续指控中国在美国及其他国家的关键交通、电力和水利基础设施中预埋恶意软件。美国网络司令部司令兼国家安全局局长蒂莫西・D・霍表示，“伏特台风” 等与中国有关联的黑客组织，正试图获取相关能力，以便在可能涉及中国的军事突发事件中，干扰外国的军事介入行动。

未来的中美人工智能安全双边对话，可能会有两国更多政府部门（包括国防部）的高层官员参与。在中美两国安全领域的军控与风险管控对话中纳入人工智能议题，或许会产生积极效果。近期，两国已同意恢复直接军事对话。

尽管如此，中美短期内不太可能就人工智能问题谈判达成正式条约。即便是两国同步发表的单边声明，也会面临履约核查与监督的难题。鉴于中美以往危机沟通机制存在诸多问题，设立人工智能突发事件热线的实际价值也有待商榷。

在人工智能与军事安全领域，最富成效的对话或将继续在非官方的二轨对话中开展，这类对话更注重实践层面的探讨，而非原则性的争论。以往的专家交流活动已经在术语界定、案例研讨等方面取得进展，尤其是通过模拟人工智能相关冲突场景，收获了宝贵成果。未来的对话可以吸纳学术界与产业界代表参与，进一步提升对话实效。

无论如何，中美两国仍将继续围绕人工智能议题保持接触 —— 毕竟两国拥有全球规模最大的人工智能企业与市场。与此同时，人工智能技术兼具商业核心地位与军民两用属性，这一特质决定了该议题将持续成为中美之间的矛盾焦点。

*China and the United States held their first intergovernmental meeting on artificial intelligence (AI) in Geneva, Switzerland on May 14, 2024.*

China and the United States held their first intergovernmental meeting on artificial intelligence (AI) on May 14. The session followed [months of preparations](https://www.eurasiareview.com/07062024-the-uss-grip-on-china-in-the-field-of-ai-will-only-tighten-further-analysis/ "months of preparations") between Chinese and U.S. representatives that culminated in a concrete commitment at last November’s Xi-Biden presidential talks during the November 2023 [Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation Summit](https://carnegieendowment.org/posts/2023/11/three-takeaways-from-the-biden-xi-meeting "Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation Summit"). As expected, the meeting did not yield concrete decisions, deliverables, or a joint statement, though both governments expressed satisfaction with an opportunity to exchange views. 

In line with the Biden administration’s efforts to build “guardrails” against military conflict, U.S. officials [termed](https://apnews.com/article/artificial-intelligence-china-united-states-biden-xi-geneva-506da7b5fa72d5fe1bcd54fb8ec4f898 "termed") such AI talks “an important part of responsibly managing competition” by helping make the world safer. Nathaniel C. Fick, the first U.S. Ambassador at Large for Cyberspace and Digital Policy, [said](https://www.state.gov/briefings-foreign-press-centers/the-united-states-international-cyberspace-and-digital-policy-strategy "said") the AI communication channel “will allow the United States and China to find areas to collaborate and work together, even as they compete in other areas.” Both governments participated in last year’s inaugural AI Safety Summit in the United Kingdom (which issued the [so-called Bletchley Declaration](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-safety-summit-2023-the-bletchley-declaration/the-bletchley-declaration-by-countries-attending-the-ai-safety-summit-1-2-november-2023 "so-called Bletchley Declaration"), signed by both China and the United States) and this May’s [follow-up summit in Seoul](<https://www.csis.org/analysis/ai-seoul-summit#:~:text=The > "follow-up summit in Seoul"). 

Secretary of State Anthony Blinken detailed the current U.S. approach toward emerging technologies in a presentation at last month’s RSA Conference in San Francisco, timed with the release of the State Department’s new [International Cyberspace and Digital Policy Strategy](https://www.state.gov/briefings-foreign-press-centers/the-united-states-international-cyberspace-and-digital-policy-strategy "International Cyberspace and Digital Policy Strategy"). 

According to U.S. officials, the strategy’s “north star – its organizing principle – is the concept of digital solidarity” among allies, partners, companies, and civil society actors sharing U.S. values. Blinken [defined](https://www.state.gov/technology-and-the-transformation-of-u-s-foreign-policy/ "defined") digital solidarity as “a willingness to work together on shared goals…to help partners build capacity to provide mutual support…to collaborate on everything from norms to regulations to technical standards.” U.S. officials argue that this focus on partnerships—also seen in Secretary of Defense Lloyd Austin’s [speech](https://www.defense.gov/News/Speeches/Speech/Article/3793580/the-new-convergence-in-the-indo-pacific-remarks-by-secretary-of-defense-lloyd-j/ "speech") at the recent Shangri-La defense dialogue—contrasts with authoritarian regimes’ prioritization of “digital sovereignty.” 

At the Geneva talks, the PRC representatives sought to reverse tightening U.S. limits on the transfer of advanced technologies to China. The Chinese government perceives the U.S. “[small yard, high fence](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefing-room/speeches-remarks/2022/10/13/remarks-by-national-security-advisor-jake-sullivan-on-the-biden-harris-administrations-national-security-strategy/ "small yard, high fence")” approach limiting exports of advanced semiconductors having potential military or AI applications as excessively broad and restrictive. They instead proposed consideration of joint China-U.S. AI-related research and development projects. 

The Chinese government also wants to strengthen the role of the United Nations in governing AI norms and rules, partly to prevent U.S. tech companies or the U.S. government from enjoying decisive influence on the issue. Such a stance, seen in the [negotiations regarding a UN declaration on a Global Digital Compact](https://www.devex.com/news/exclusive-at-un-china-seeks-greater-state-control-over-internet-107648 "negotiations regarding a UN declaration on a Global Digital Compact"), also aims to [appeal to developing countries](https://www.forbes.com/sites/johannacostigan/2024/05/20/us-china-dont-agree-on-much-at-ai-talks-but-share-ai-safety-concerns/ "appeal to developing countries") lacking leading AI firms. Though cooperating in UN bodies, U.S. officials have focused most attention on the G7, where China and Russia are not members. To compete with Beijing for Global South approval, Blinken and other U.S. officials have [stressed](https://www.state.gov/technology-and-the-transformation-of-u-s-foreign-policy/ "stressed") their affirmative vision for using AI to impart momentum to lagging [UN Sustainable Development](https://sdgs.un.org/goals "UN Sustainable Development") [Goals](https://sdgs.un.org/goals "Goals"). 

In a presentation at the Washington Post headquarters on June 6, Fick [said](https://www.washingtonpost.com/washington-post-live/2024/06/06/transcript-securing-cyberspace/ "said") the United States was interested in continuing its dialogue with China. For example, both governments can exchange information about how they are aligning frontier AI systems with human values. Though the U.S. approach currently focuses more on securing “voluntary commitments” by developers to subject novel technologies to rigorous safety and security reviews, which Flik valued for their flexibility in addressing rapid technological changes, the United States may move closer to the more regulatory approach of PRC authorities. 

Still, the range and impact of these discussions will remain limited. Beijing has declined to sign the U.S.-authored [Political Declaration on Responsible Military Use of Artificial Intelligence and Autonomy,](https://www.state.gov/political-declaration-on-responsible-military-use-of-artificial-intelligence-and-autonomy/ "Political Declaration on Responsible Military Use of Artificial Intelligence and Autonomy,") which more than 50 other governments support but needs more backing to become an established norm of responsible international behavior. 

The United States is unlikely to relax national security export and investment controls for U.S. technologies having military application. For the same reason, U.S. officials will continue to resist collaborating on technical development projects and [may](https://www.reuters.com/technology/us-eyes-curbs-chinas-access-ai-software-behind-apps-like-chatgpt-2024-05-08/ "may") curtail Chinese access to U.S.-developed closed-source AI models. They also want to prevent foreign actors from exploiting AI technologies to interfere in U.S. elections. Analysts are [concerned](https://www.wired.com/story/indian-elections-ai-deepfakes/ "concerned") that AI tools will make such cyber manipulation [easier and more effective](https://www.axios.com/2024/05/30/openai-misinformation-china-israel-russia-iran "easier and more effective"). For example, AI might make “deepfakes” of politicians more realistic, enable precision targeting of specially constructed communications to discrete individuals, and make foreign messaging appear more authentic. 

Furthermore, U.S. officials will [continue](https://www.state.gov/briefings-foreign-press-centers/the-united-states-international-cyberspace-and-digital-policy-strategy "continue") to raise allegations that China is pre-positioning malware in critical transportation, power, and water infrastructure in the United States and other countries. Timothy D. Haugh, commander of the U.S. Cyber Command and director of the National Security Agency, [said](https://www.wsj.com/politics/national-security/china-is-prepositioning-for-future-cyberattacksand-thenew-nsa-chief-is-worried-5ede04ef?mod=mhp "said") that [Volt Typhoon](https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/cybersecurity-advisories/aa24-038a "Volt Typhoon") and other PRC-linked groups sought options to disrupt foreign military intervention in potential military contingencies involving China. 

Future bilateral talks on AI safety may involve higher-level officials from a broader range of government agencies, including defense departments. It could be [beneficial](https://www.rand.org/pubs/commentary/2024/04/building-a-foundation-for-strategic-stability-with.html "beneficial") to address AI issues in arms control and risk reduction dialogue between the Chinese and U.S. security communities. The two governments recently agreed to resume direct military talks. 

Nonetheless, China and the United States are unlikely to negotiate a formal AI treaty anytime soon, while unilateral statements, even if made in parallel by both governments, will confront [problems of verifying and enforcing compliance](https://www.wsj.com/articles/iaea-for-ai-that-model-has-already-failed-chaptgpt-technology-nuclear-proliferation-4339543b "problems of verifying and enforcing compliance"). Establishing an [AI Incidents Hotline](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/the-u.s.-and-china-need-an-ai-incidents-hotline "AI Incidents Hotline") is of questionable value given [problems](https://www.chinausfocus.com/foreign-policy/history-suggests-the-balloon-incident-bodes-ill-for-sino-american-relations-in-2023 "problems") with past Sino-American crisis communication mechanisms. 

The most productive talks on [AI and military security](https://nationalinterest.org/feature/will-artificial-intelligence-lead-war-208958 "AI and military security") will likely continue to occur in [non-governmental (Track II) dialogues](https://defensescoop.com/2024/05/20/us-china-track-2-dialogue-ai-experts-meeting-thailand/ "non-governmental (Track II) dialogues") concentrating on practice rather than principles. [Past expert exchanges](https://www.chinausfocus.com/peace-security/opportunities-in-dialogues-regarding-ai "Past expert exchanges") have clarified terminology, reviewed case studies, and, especially valuable, simulated potential AI-related conflict scenarios. Future sessions can profitably [encompass academic and industry representatives](https://www.chinausfocus.com/peace-security/more-voices-would-help-ai-dialogue "encompass academic and industry representatives"). 

One way or another, the two countries will continue engaging with each other on AI issues since they possess the largest AI companies and markets. At the same time, the commercial centrality of AI technology and its inherent dual-use nature ensure that AI issues will remain a source of tension between China and the United States.
