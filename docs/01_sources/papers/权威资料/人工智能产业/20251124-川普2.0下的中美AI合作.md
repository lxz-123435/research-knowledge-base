# 特朗普政府二度执政背景下的中美人工智能合作

2025 年 11 月 24 日作者：凯文・沃巴赫

**区域**：[印太地区](https://perryworldhouse.upenn.edu/news-and-insight/?exposed_taxonomy_region\[0]=24 "印太地区")、[北美地区](https://perryworldhouse.upenn.edu/news-and-insight/?exposed_taxonomy_region\[0]=26 "北美地区")**主题**：[安全](https://perryworldhouse.upenn.edu/news-and-insight/?exposed_taxonomy_topic\[0]=28 "安全")、[技术](https://perryworldhouse.upenn.edu/news-and-insight/?exposed_taxonomy_topic\[0]=61 "技术")

本文与宾大佩里世界事务中心报告《人工智能治理的未来与国际政治》同步发布。

作为全球两大经济、军事和科技超级大国，中美两国均认识到，人工智能将是塑造未来数十年全球格局的核心技术，其重要性毋庸置疑。任何一方都无法单独应对人工智能带来的风险。唐纳德・特朗普再度执政后，美国政府高调反对全球人工智能治理框架，这势必导致中国在国际组织中的影响力上升，但与此同时，这些国际组织的自身影响力可能会被削弱。人工智能监管领域的艰巨挑战，需要全球人工智能研发与应用领域的两大主导力量直接参与其中。

## 人工智能领域的竞争本质是什么？

拜登政府将人工智能竞争视为一场 “可引领但未必能必胜” 的赛跑。一方面，政府通过《芯片与科学法案》以及后来被撤销的《人工智能扩散管控规则》，尽可能延缓中国前沿人工智能技术的发展进程；另一方面，着力推动美国国内 “安全、可靠、可信” 的人工智能技术研发。拜登在 2023 年签署的同名行政令中明确指出：“要利用人工智能造福人类，充分发挥其诸多优势，就必须化解其带来的重大风险。” 若人工智能竞争没有明确的终点，最优策略就是在采取一切措施保持本土优势的同时，携手应对两国共同面临的人工智能挑战。

有趣的是，这一理念与中国对人工智能的认知不谋而合。中国领导人习近平强调，中国要 “在人工智能领域抢占先机、赢得主动”，而非将其定义为一场 “非赢即输” 的战争。在中国，顶尖人工智能开发者对通用人工智能的关注度相对较低，他们将人工智能视为一种手段而非最终目标。2025 年夏季，特朗普政府发布《美国人工智能行动计划》后不久，中国也出台了 “人工智能 +” 行动框架，将人工智能定位为 “国际竞争的新焦点和经济发展的强劲引擎”。与此同时，中国在人工智能立法、公共人工智能聊天机器人等系统的部署要求以及人工智能伦理准则制定方面，采取了比美国更为积极主动的举措。

特朗普政府则秉持截然不同的理念。尽管两届政府的政策实质存在诸多共性，与二者风格迥异、言辞尖锐的表象形成反差，但特朗普政府二度执政后的人工智能政策核心逻辑十分明确：人工智能竞争是一场 “能赢且必须赢” 的赛跑。其发布的《美国人工智能行动计划》副标题即为 “赢得竞争”，并高调宣称：“赢得人工智能竞赛，将开创人类繁荣发展、经济竞争制胜、国家安全稳固的全新黄金时代。”

若将赢得人工智能竞赛置于首要位置，那么保障人工智能应用的无偏性、隐私保护性和低误导性就会退居次要地位，除非这些要素直接影响竞赛结果。正因如此，特朗普政府不仅叫停了美国国内的人工智能治理相关举措，还抨击其他国家和跨国组织在该领域推进协同治理的努力。原本由美国国家标准与技术研究院主导的人工智能安全研究机构全球合作网络，以及人工智能治理标准推广工作，都被收缩范围，仅聚焦于网络安全和人工智能系统对齐等与国家安全直接相关的议题 —— 毕竟这些领域一旦出现漏洞，可能引发灾难性后果，危及国家安全。

与此同时，特朗普政府的政策导向促使美国放宽了中美人工智能企业间的商业合作限制。近期达成的 TikTok 平台控制权转让协议就是典型例证，尽管中国此前曾明确表示排除此类交易的可能性。有人或许将这一转变完全归因于商业利益驱动，但事实上，该决策与特朗普政府的整体世界观一脉相承。

在特朗普政府看来，如果人工智能竞争是一场决定全球主导权的赛跑，那么当前中美人工智能生态系统相互隔离的状态只是暂时的，最终的竞争终点才是关键。因此，政府虽未完全取消《芯片与科学法案》的限制措施，但更倾向于推动美国人工智能基础设施的全球推广，即便这些设施被中国实体或美国势力范围之外的国家所使用。白宫人工智能高级政策顾问斯瑞拉姆・克里希南（曾任安德森・霍洛维茨风险投资公司合伙人）曾表示，这场竞争本质上是两大 “人工智能技术栈” 的对决，美国的目标是 “让全球范围内运行在美国硬件上的美国人工智能模型，处理尽可能多的数据交互量”。特朗普政府认为，此举不仅能助力美国在创新领域抢占先机，还能使其掌握对日益强大的人工智能技术的控制权。

由此，“美国人工智能” 的定义发生了转变：不再局限于美国盟友或认同美国价值观、承诺融入美国势力范围的国家所开发的技术，而是特指运行在美国主导的底层架构（主要是半导体和前沿模型）之上的人工智能技术。特朗普政府二度执政后推行的全新人工智能政策，彻底改变了中美人工智能合作的格局。美国此前在全球范围内推动私营企业落实人工智能责任与治理标准的举措已被搁置，尽管欧盟和部分国际组织仍在继续推进相关工作。不过，两届政府在人工智能领域仍存在一个延续性的合作方向，同时还涌现出四个新的合作契机。

## 前沿人工智能安全领域

冷战时期，美国与苏联尽管处于敌对状态，仍能在防止核战争意外升级、构建核不扩散机制、限制生化武器使用等领域找到共同利益契合点。核心原因在于，双方均认同核误射等极端场景将带来毁灭性后果。

在人工智能领域，与之对应的合作方向就是前沿人工智能安全。随着人工智能系统的能力不断提升，其因技术故障或被恶意行为体利用而引发灾难性风险的可能性也日益增加。2024 年底，中美两国元首达成协议，明确核打击指令的最终决策权必须由人类掌控。除此之外，中美两国在政府和非政府层面已建立多个交流渠道。例如，“人工智能安全国际对话” 机制汇聚了两国顶尖人工智能科学家与学者，通过在北京、上海等地举办系列会议，探讨人工智能带来的极端风险。中国于今年成立了人工智能安全研究机构，与其他国家的同类机构形成对应，为开展技术协作搭建了平台。中美两国在确保先进人工智能系统的行为符合开发者预期这一问题上，有着共同的利益诉求。

但现实挑战在于，前沿人工智能技术的许多高危应用场景，同时具备极高的军事和情报价值。例如，利用人工智能发起零日漏洞网络攻击、凭借人工智能的超强说服力策反情报人员、通过提示词注入等对抗性技术干扰敌方人工智能系统等。这些技术一旦被敌对国家或组织掌握，将构成严重威胁，因此中美两国均不可能放弃自身在相关领域的研发与应用能力。

## 开源人工智能领域

当前，中美两国在支持开源（技术层面特指 “开放权重”）人工智能研发方面的立场正趋于一致。值得注意的是，这一趋势目前尚未延伸至美国主流人工智能实验室。元宇宙平台公司（Meta）是唯一一家坚持开源其最先进模型的企业，但近期有迹象表明其正逐步淡化这一立场。不过，美国的前沿人工智能实验室仍会开源一些性能相对有限的模型。在中国，深度求索（DeepSeek）等顶尖人工智能实验室已全面投身开源领域，阿里巴巴、百度等科技巨头也在政府的引导下，转向开源其最优模型。

开源人工智能技术的全球发展，为中美两国在标准制定和许可授权等议题上开展合作创造了条件。针对威胁行为体滥用高性能开源模型的风险防范问题开展联合研究，同样具有重要价值。例如，能否为人工智能模型植入 “终止开关” 或数字指纹技术，从而追溯恶意行为体所使用的模型来源？

## 算法治理领域

两国当前的执政理念，使得算法治理成为另一个潜在的合作领域。在许多西方国家看来，政府大规模运用人工智能开展社会工程的前景令人担忧。以《欧盟人工智能法案》为例，其第 5 条明确将社会信用评分、执法部门无差别使用生物识别技术等行为列为 “禁止性应用”。而在中国，算法治理已有较长的实践历史，作为多元化社会信用体系的重要组成部分，算法治理始终备受重视。政府广泛应用人脸识别技术，并创新性地运用人工智能手段落实在新疆地区针对维吾尔族群体的相关政策。特朗普政府二度执政后，其新设的政府效能部大力推动数据整合工作，并运用人工智能技术改革联邦政府机构的运作模式。美国移民与海关执法局等执法机构也在积极推动人脸识别和生成式人工智能技术的应用，以强化执法效能。

若美国在算法治理路径上的立场，与中国的契合度超过与多数西方民主国家的契合度，那么两国可能迎来协作契机，例如共同抵制限制政府运用算法技术的相关举措，以及开展执法领域的信息共享合作。

## 人工智能评估领域

第三个潜在合作方向是，共同开发并标准化人工智能系统评估机制。英国和新加坡已将此类评估机制列为国家人工智能治理战略的核心内容。与此同时，美国（通过商务部下属的国家标准与技术研究院开展相关工作）和中国（通过国家互联网信息办公室监管的聊天机器人许可评估制度，以及法律强制要求的算法备案机制）也在该领域深耕多年，积累了丰富经验。

鉴于人工智能平台的全球性特征，有效的评估机制必须实现标准化并具备跨境适用性，这需要政府与私营机构协同参与。中美两国在该领域的合作，将主要聚焦于技术层面，或围绕评估标准的互认展开磋商。

## 监管创新领域

最后一个出人意料的合作领域是人工智能治理的制度设计创新。传统监管模式难以适配人工智能技术的发展需求：技术迭代速度过快、应用场景高度嵌入社会各领域、发展过程存在诸多不确定性，这些因素决定了僵化的监管规则无法奏效。在保障人工智能技术创新不受过度制约的前提下化解其潜在风险，至关重要 —— 尤其是在人工智能发展对提升国家全球竞争力具有决定性意义的当下。正因如此，世界各国都在积极探索人工智能治理的法律与制度创新路径。

特朗普政府对全面人工智能监管持怀疑态度，国会共和党议员曾试图推动立法，要求各州暂停出台人工智能相关法律，禁令期限长达十年。不过，右翼阵营的核心人士也提出了一系列创新性监管提案，例如乔希・霍利参议员提出的人工智能企业责任法案、泰德・克鲁兹参议员提出的监管豁免方案，以及《美国人工智能行动计划》主笔人迪恩・鲍尔提出的私营主体治理模式。中国同样展现出探索创新治理模式的意愿，2025 年初，有学者起草了人工智能立法草案，为后续制度设计提供了讨论蓝本。2025 年 5 月，我于北京大学联合主办了一场全球人工智能治理学术研讨会，中美两国学者齐聚一堂，围绕应对人工智能全新挑战的创新方案展开探讨，双方均表现出浓厚兴趣。

## 结语

能够推动中美人工智能对话的举措，往往是一些成熟且务实的路径：即便在敌对状态下，也要努力寻找共同利益契合点；同时，搭建两国专家交流网络。此外，先进人工智能领域的双极格局能否长期维持，在技术和经济层面仍存在较大不确定性。但总体而言，当前中美两国在人工智能领域看似分歧加剧，实则在若干关键领域的立场正在悄然趋同。

## 作者简介

凯文・沃巴赫，宾夕法尼亚大学沃顿商学院廖烈武 / 第一太平集团讲席教授，法律研究与商业伦理系主任。

This article was published alongside the PWH Report “The Future of Artificial Intelligence Governance and International Politics.”

The United States and China, as the world’s two economic, military, and technological superpowers, recognize the paramount importance of artificial intelligence (AI) as the technology that will shape global affairs in the coming decades. Neither can fully address its risks alone. With the United States, under President Donald Trump, aggressively [dismissing](https://usun.usmission.gov/remarks-at-the-security-councils-open-debate-on-artificial-intelligence-and-international-peace-and-security/ "dismissing") global governance for AI, the influence of China within international organizations is bound to increase. But the influence of those organizations may decrease. Hard challenges of AI oversight will require direct involvement of both dominant players in AI development and deployment.  

## What Kind of Race is AI? 

The Biden administration viewed AI as a race that could be led, though not necessarily won. Through the [CHIPS Act](https://www.cfr.org/in-brief/what-chips-act "CHIPS Act") and since-rescinded [Diffusion Rule](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/01/15/2025-00636/framework-for-artificial-intelligence-diffusion "Diffusion Rule"), it sought to delay Chinese frontier AI development to the extent possible. At the same time, it worked to promote “Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy” AI development domestically. As \[then-U.S. President Joe] Biden’s 2023 [Executive Order](https://bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov/briefing-room/presidential-actions/2023/10/30/executive-order-on-the-safe-secure-and-trustworthy-development-and-use-of-artificial-intelligence/ "Executive Order") with that title states: “Harnessing AI for good and realizing its myriad benefits requires mitigating its substantial risks.” If the AI race has no visible endpoint, the best strategy is to take whatever steps can help maintain local advantage, while working to address the challenges of AI that both nations face.  

Interestingly, this vision mirrors the way China appears to think about AI. Chinese leader Xi Jinping has [exhorted](https://www.gov.cn/yaowen/liebiao/202504/content_7021072.htm "exhorted") China to “take the lead and win advantages in the field of artificial intelligence,” rather than adopting a framing of winning or losing the war. For China, where the vision of artificial general intelligence (AGI) is less central in the minds of leading AI developers, AI is a means rather than an end. China’s [AI+ framework](https://www.gov.cn/zhengce/202508/content_7037929.htm "AI+ framework"), released shortly after the Trump administration’s own [AI Action Plan](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Americas-AI-Action-Plan.pdf "AI Action Plan") in summer 2025, describes AI as “a new focus of international competition and a powerful engine of economic development.” At the same time, China has been more aggressive than the United States in adopting laws governing AI, requirements for deployments of systems such as public-facing AI chatbots, and ethical guidelines for AI. 

The Trump administration has a different vision. While there is more commonality between the two administrations on the substance than it might seem from their radically different style and rhetoric, the basic calculus of Trump 2.0 AI policy is that AI is a race that can and should be won. Its AI Action Plan is subtitled, “Winning the Race,” and it boldly [declares](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Americas-AI-Action-Plan.pdf "declares") that, “Winning the AI race will usher in a new golden age of human flourishing, economic competitiveness, and national security.” 

If winning the AI race is paramount, ensuring the AI we deploy is free from bias, privacy protective, and less-prone to misinformation, is less important, except to the extent it bears on the outcome of the race. Thus, the Trump administration has not only [rolled back](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/01/removing-barriers-to-american-leadership-in-artificial-intelligence/ "rolled back") U.S. efforts on AI governance, it has [attacked](https://www.politico.eu/article/jd-vance-waging-war-eu-tech-law-msc-ai-summit/ "attacked") other countries and multinational organizations seeking coordination in those areas. The global infrastructure of AI Safety Institutes and the efforts under NIST to promote AI governance standards have been [narrowed](https://fedscoop.com/trump-administration-rebrands-ai-safety-institute-aisi-caisi/ "narrowed") to focus on cybersecurity and alignment topics. These are areas where there is recognition that catastrophic harms might occur, undermining national security. 

At the same time, the Trump approach has led the United States to accept greater commercial cooperation between United States and Chinese AI providers. Hence, the recent [deal](https://www.npr.org/2025/09/22/nx-s1-5550152/tiktok-deal-joint-venture "deal") to transfer the Chinese social media platform TikTok to U.S. control, despite prior Chinese [statements](https://www.cnn.com/2024/04/25/business/bytedance-denies-reports-selling-tiktok-intl-hnk "statements") ruling out such an option. While some may attribute this entirely to commercial interests, the shift is actually consistent with a broader worldview.  

If AI is a global race to be won, with the winner dominating the world, the siloed configuration of U.S. and Chinese ecosystems is only temporary. All that matters is the endpoint. The Trump administration, while not entirely removing the CHIPS Act restrictions, is more interested in promoting the diffusion of U.S. AI infrastructure, even if utilized by Chinese entities or other nations outside the US sphere of influence. In the [words](https://x.com/sriramk/status/1961072926561550366 "words") of White House Senior Policy Advisor for AI Sriram Krishnan, a former venture capitalist at the firm Andreessen Horowitz, the race is between two “AI stacks,” and the U.S. objective is “maximizing tokens inferenced by American models running on American hardware all over the world.” This, the Trump administration believes, will give the United States a leg up on innovation and also maintain its leverage to exercise control over ever-more-powerful AI.   

“American AI” is therefore now conceptualized as AI running on US-controlled foundations (principally semiconductors and frontier models), rather than AI from U.S. allies and others who accept substantive commitments to join the US sphere. The new U.S. approach to AI in the second Trump administration changes the landscape for potential U.S.-China cooperation. Out go the efforts to standardize and gain commitments globally from the private sector on AI responsibility and governance, although the European Union and international organizations will continue their efforts in those areas. One area for cooperation remains across the two administrations. Four new opportunities emerge. 

## Frontier AI Safety 

Even during the Cold War, the United States and the Soviet Union found shared interests such as preventing accidental nuclear escalation, creating a regime to restrain nuclear proliferation, and limiting chemical and biological weapons. The key opportunity was that both sides, despite being adversaries, agreed that scenarios such as a nuclear launch due to a misunderstanding would have devastating consequences.  

In the AI context, the analogue is frontier AI safety. Increasingly powerful AI systems [run the risk](https://crfm.stanford.edu/assets/report.pdf "run the risk") of facilitating catastrophic harms, either through accidents or use by threat actors. In late 2024, Biden and Xi signed an [agreement](https://www.reuters.com/world/biden-xi-agreed-that-humans-not-ai-should-control-nuclear-weapons-white-house-2024-11-16/ "agreement") committing that only a human could launch a nuclear strike. Beyond this, there are already a number of governmental and non-governmental touch points between the United States and China. For example, the [International Dialogues on AI Safety](https://idais.ai/ "International Dialogues on AI Safety") bring together top AI scientists and academics from the two countries, in a series of meetings to address extreme AI risks, including sessions in Beijing and Shanghai. China has also this year established a [counterpart](https://carnegieendowment.org/research/2025/06/how-some-of-chinas-top-ai-thinkers-built-their-own-ai-safety-institute "counterpart") to the AI safety institutes that promote technical collaboration among nations. Both the United States and China have a stake in ensuring that advanced AI systems do not act in ways their creators did not intend. 

The challenge is that many other dangerous uses of frontier AI also have military or intelligence value. Use of AI to create zero-day cyber attacks, or to recruit assets through AI’s capability to be hyper-persuasive, or to disrupt enemy AI systems through prompt injection or other adversarial techniques, are all threatening when done by hostile states or groups. Neither the United States nor China is likely to give up their own ability to do so.   

## Open Source 

The two countries are now [converging](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/chinas-lead-in-open-source-ai-jolts-washington-and-silicon-valley-ffdec83b?gaa_at=eafs\&gaa_n=ASWzDAjDvGFrObF2EvP_6HalRLBLcD5kw0Xq3-PZ5dPfacxLe4q1Gsz5nIFK_uk-ofY=\&gaa_ts=68e923dd\&gaa_sig=mnd43Tvl1XtdhRX0I4nlQWKtTvbRHvigukhiSdg4qpEKYzZutO8bv6fjY-Ir5BbUcZ6gH_6DIvr23_CQ6B4Hcw== "converging") on support for open source (technically open weights) AI development. Interestingly, this view so far does not extend to U.S. AI labs. Meta is the only one with a strong commitment to open sourcing its most powerful models, and appears to be [stepping back](https://fortune.com/2025/07/31/zuckerberg-meta-open-source-risks-superintelligence/ "stepping back") from that position. However, the U.S. frontier AI labs do release less-powerful AI models as open source. In China, the leading AI labs such as DeepSeek are all-in on open source, and the major firms such as Alibaba and Baidu have shifted toward open sourcing their best models, no doubt with the encouragement of the Chinese state.  

A world with more open-source AI will create opportunities for U.S.-China cooperation on standards and licensing questions. Research on thorny issues of how to address risks from powerful open-source models in the hands of threat actors would also be valuable. For example, is it possible to implement “kill switches” or digital fingerprints that would allow tracing of which AI models were used by hostile actors?  

## Algorithmic Governance 

A second area where the two sides may find common ground with their current leadership is algorithmic governance. For many in the West, the prospect of governments using AI aggressively for social engineering is terrifying. The European Union’s AI Act, for example, includes social scoring and indiscriminate use of biometric identification by law enforcement among the small list of “prohibited practices” in [Article 5](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/5/ "Article 5"). In China, however, algorithmic governance has a long history, and received [significant attention](https://illinoislawreview.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/Werbach.pdf "significant attention") as part of the multi-faceted Social Credit initiative. Facial recognition is widely used by the government, and AI has been used creatively to enforce Chinese policies toward the Uyghur Muslim minority in Xinjiang. In the second Trump administration, the erstwhile Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) aggressively pushed for [data consolidation](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/privacy-under-siege-doges-one-big-beautiful-database/ "data consolidation") and use of AI to transform the operation of federal agencies. Law enforcement agencies such as Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) are [pushing](https://www.wired.com/story/ice-social-media-surveillance-24-7-contract/ "pushing") the use of facial recognition and generative AI in seeking to ramp up their activities.   

If the United States finds itself more closely aligned with the Chinese approach to algorithmic governance than that of most other Western democracies, it may create opportunities for coordination between the two countries. These could include pushback on efforts to limit governmental uses along these lines and information sharing between law enforcement.   

## Assessments 

A third area of potential collaboration lies in developing and standardizing mechanisms for evaluating AI systems. The [United Kingdom](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/trusted-third-party-ai-assurance-roadmap/trusted-third-party-ai-assurance-roadmap "United Kingdom") and [Singapore](https://assurance.aiverifyfoundation.sg/report/introduction/ "Singapore"), in particular, have focused on such measures as a central aspect of their national AI governance strategies. However, the United States (through [work](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework "work") under the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) at the Department of Commerce) and China (through chatbot [licensing assessments](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/07/13/china-introduces-rules-governing-generative-ai-services-like-chatgpt.html "licensing assessments") supervised by the Cyberspace Administration of China, and an “[algorithm registry](https://www.chinatalk.media/p/sb-1047-with-socialist-characteristics "algorithm registry")” mechanism required by law) have also done significant work.  

Ultimately, to be effective, AI assessments will need to be standardized and able to function across borders, given the global nature of AI platforms. They will involve a combination of governmental and private entities. Cooperation between the United States and China in this area would largely be technical in nature, or would involve approval of standards for local acceptance of assessments.  

## Regulatory Innovation 

A final surprising area for collaboration is in institutional design for AI governance. Traditional forms of regulation may not be effective for AI. The speed of change is too fast; AI will be embedded into so many activities, and there are too many uncertainties to adopt rigid rules. Addressing AI’s dangers without overly slowing innovation is also critical, especially when AI development is so critical to global competitiveness. As a result, countries around the world are experimenting with legal and governance approaches to AI.  

The Trump administration is skeptical of broad AI regulation, and Republicans in Congress attempted to pass a [ten-year moratorium](https://www.reuters.com/legal/government/us-senate-strikes-ai-regulation-ban-trump-megabill-2025-07-01/ "ten-year moratorium") on state AI laws. However, key figures on the right have floated novel proposals for AI regulatory regimes, including Senator Josh Hawley’s [proposals on liability for AI providers](https://www.hawley.senate.gov/hawley-durbin-introduce-legislation-empowering-americans-to-bring-liability-claims-against-ai-companies/ "proposals on liability for AI providers"), Senator Ted Cruz’s [regulatory waiver proposal](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2025/9/sen-cruz-unveils-ai-policy-framework-to-strengthen-american-ai-leadership "regulatory waiver proposal"), and U.S. AI Action Plan lead author Dean Ball’s [private governance](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2504.11501 "private governance") concept. China has also shown a willingness to explore new ideas, including a [scholar’s draft of AI legislation](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.ady7922 "scholar’s draft of AI legislation") that was put forth as a stalking horse in early 2025. I co-organized an academic workshop on global AI governance at Peking University in May 2025, with scholars from both the United States and China, and found great interest on both sides in new ideas for addressing AI’s novel challenges.

## Conclusion 

The kinds of initiatives that might increase the likelihood of U.S.-China AI dialogue are likely to be familiar ones: finding shared interests even among adversaries and building networks among experts on both sides. And there remains significant technological and economic uncertainty as to whether the bipolar configuration of advanced AI will endure. Overall, though, we seem to be moving into a period where the United States and China seem further apart regarding AI, but may actually be closer than it seems in key areas.
